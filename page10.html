About Anthropocene

Anthropocene, a new term combined by “anthropo” (means human) and “-cene” (means a new epoch), was first introduced by Dutch atmospheric chemist Paul Crutzen in 2000. It is proposed to begin during the Industrial Revolution in the 18th century when the actions of human started to put negative impacts on the climate, eco-system and geology on Earth. It is not a synonym of “environmental problem”, but a “catastrophe” in the modern world.

The honeybee, which is stated to be an essential agent for pollination in apiculture development and to be “the glue that holds our agricultural system together", is being pushed to the verge of extinction in the Anthropocene due to human’s excessive use of chemical pesticide and environmental pollution. Across the globe, there are 100 species of plants that compose 90 percent of the food supply. Seventy-one of these are bee-pollinated. This implies that if there is mass extinction of honeybees, humans are likely to suffer from significant reduction in food supply. Maurice Maeterlinck, Belgian playwright, famously stated more than a century ago, “if bees were to vanish from the globe, humans would have only four years left to live” and the quote became more popular.
